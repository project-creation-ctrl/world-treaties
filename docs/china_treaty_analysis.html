Section 04 — Strategic Consequences

How China's Treaties Impact India

Each Chinese treaty signed in India's neighbourhood is a diplomatic move on a geopolitical chessboard. The cumulative effect is strategic encirclement.

HIGH THREAT
🔴

CPEC — Territorial Dispute

China-Pakistan Economic Corridor passes through Gilgit-Baltistan — territory India claims. The $62B project gives China permanent infrastructure in a disputed zone and a direct Arabian Sea port (Gwadar), bypassing India entirely.

HIGH THREAT
⚓

String of Pearls — Naval Encirclement

China secured port access in Gwadar (Pakistan), Hambantota (Sri Lanka), and Chittagong (Bangladesh) through infrastructure treaties. Each port is a potential dual-use naval facility surrounding India in the Indian Ocean.

HIGH THREAT
💱

Trade Deficit — $53 Billion

India's trade deficit with China stands at ~$53B. China's FTA network makes its goods cheaper globally, undercutting Indian manufacturing. India refused to join RCEP partly for this reason.

MEDIUM THREAT
🇧🇩

Neighbourhood Capture

Bangladesh, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Maldives and Myanmar have all signed major BRI agreements. China's economic penetration of India's neighbours pressures India's Neighbourhood First Policy and traditional regional dominance.

MEDIUM THREAT
🛰️

Digital Silk Road

China is building undersea cables, satellite networks and 5G infrastructure across South Asia. These digital treaties create technological dependency and intelligence-sharing vulnerabilities for India.

INDIA RESPONDING
🏗️

India's Counter — Chabahar & IMEC

India built Chabahar Port (Iran) as a direct counter to Gwadar. The India-Middle East-Europe Corridor (IMEC) offers an alternative BRI route. India also launched its own Neighbourhood First Policy.

Section 05 — Doctrinal Analysis

China vs India — Treaty Strategy Compared

Beyond the numbers, the philosophies of treaty-making differ fundamentally. China treats international law as a tool; India treats it as a framework.

Dimension 🇨🇳 China 🇮🇳 India
Treaty Philosophy Functionalist — sign what serves strategic interests Normative multilateralism — sign based on principles
FTAs in Force 23 (as of 2024) ~13 (more selective)
BITs Signed 149 (second only to Germany) ~80 (scaled back after 2016 model BIT revision)
Key Regional Strategy BRI — infrastructure diplomacy + debt leverage Neighbourhood First + SAARC multilateralism
Approach to RCEP Key architect and member Withdrew in 2019 (fear of Chinese import surge)
Nuclear Treaties NPT member; helped Pakistan nuclear program Not NPT signatory; US-India nuclear deal (2008)
Climate Treaties Paris Agreement (signed); world's largest emitter Paris Agreement (signed); more ambitious NDCs
Selective Non-Ratification Signed CCPR (1998) but never ratified Signed most major human rights treaties
Dispute Settlement Rejects ICJ/UNCLOS rulings (South China Sea 2016) Generally accepts international arbitration
Sovereignty Doctrine Absolute — "non-interference" as shield Conditional — engaged multilateralist
PIL Doctrine Connection

"China invokes pacta sunt servanda when convenient — demanding treaty compliance from others — while strategically delaying ratification of treaties that constrain its own sovereignty. This is the classic tension between consent-based international obligation and political will."
