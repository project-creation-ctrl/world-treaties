Section 01 — Historical Record

India–Oman Treaty Timeline: 1955–2025

India and Oman share millennia of trade and people-to-people ties. The modern treaty framework began with India's consulate in 1955 and has deepened into a full strategic partnership — crowned by the 2025 CEPA which is India's first comprehensive trade deal in the Gulf after UAE.

The 2025 CEPA — negotiated in record time — reflects a convergence of economic complementarity and geopolitical calculation: Oman sits astride the Strait of Hormuz, the jugular vein of global energy trade."

Section 03 — Trade & Economic Patterns

What the Numbers Reveal

India–Oman trade crossed $10.6 billion in FY 2024–25. The CEPA is set to radically alter the composition of this trade — shifting it from energy-dominated to high-value manufacturing and services. But structural challenges remain: India runs a trade deficit dominated by crude oil.

Section 04 — Defence & Strategic Cooperation

The Defence Pillar: A Gulf First

Oman was the first Gulf country to formalise defence ties with India (2006) and remains the only Gulf country with which India conducts exercises across all three services. This makes Oman not just a trading partner but India's most critical maritime anchor in the western Indian Ocean.

Army
⚔️
Exercise Al Najah (Army)
Bilateral army exercise conducted regularly in both countries. Focuses on counter-terrorism, joint tactics, and infantry operations. Reflects shared concern over Gulf region instability and maritime threats.
Air Force
✈️
Exercise Eastern Bridge (Air Force)
Bilateral air exercise. India's IAF and Royal Air Force of Oman practise joint air operations. Strategically significant given Oman's location controlling the approach to the Strait of Hormuz.
Navy
⚓
Exercise Naseem Al Bahr (Navy)
Launched in 1993 — one of India's oldest bilateral naval exercises. Covers harbour operations, sea phase, and debrief. Vital for Indian Ocean maritime security coordination and counter-piracy operations.
Strategic
🌊
Joint Maritime Vision Document
Signed Dec 2025 during PM Modi's visit. Establishes a common framework for maritime heritage, security, and cooperation in the Indian Ocean Region — elevating the bilateral into a maritime strategic partnership.
Geopolitical
🗺️
Strait of Hormuz Access
Oman controls both sides of the Strait of Hormuz. India's defence partnership gives it an informal stake in the security of this chokepoint through which 20% of global oil trade passes — critical for India's energy security.
Cooperation
🏫
NDC–IDSA Cooperation
MoU between Oman's National Defence College and India's Institute for Defence Studies and Analyses. Builds strategic and academic defence cooperation through think-tank level engagement and exchange programmes.
Strategic Pattern

"India's military cooperation with Oman predates its strategic partnerships with most Gulf states. The tri-service exercise framework — unique in the Gulf — reflects Oman's role as India's maritime sentinel. In PIL terms, these MoUs are 'soft law' instruments: non-binding but operationally crucial, building interoperability through practice rather than treaty obligation."

Section 05 — PIL Analysis & Alignment Index

Treaty Patterns: What Do They Mean?

India–Oman treaties reveal a consistent preference for bilateral, consent-based engagement over multilateral obligation. India's treaty strategy with Oman is incrementalist — building depth through MoUs before formalising under binding treaties. The CEPA represents the culmination of decades of this approach.

PIL Doctrine — Pacta Sunt Servanda & Consent

"India–Oman treaties operate on the classic Public International Law principle of pacta sunt servanda — agreements must be honoured. The CEPA's binding nature under WTO GATS and GATT frameworks upgrades previous MoUs (soft law) into hard legal obligations. Notably, India's unilateral termination of the 1997 BIT reveals that sovereign states retain derogation rights — the CEPA's dispute resolution chapter is a deliberate attempt to constrain this through institutionalised panels rather than unilateral exit."
